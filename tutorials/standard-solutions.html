What is a standard solution?

A standard solution is a solution whose concentration is accurately and precisely known. It is the reference against which unknown samples are measured in quantitative analysis — in titrations, calibration curves, ICP standards, and more. The accuracy of every result your laboratory produces is ultimately traceable back to the quality of your standard solutions.

This tutorial explains how to prepare standard solutions correctly, the difference between primary and secondary standards, and the most common mistakes that compromise accuracy.

Primary vs secondary standards

Not all chemicals are suitable for direct preparation of accurate standards. Those that are are called primary standards. Those that need to be calibrated against a primary standard first are called secondary standards.

Primary StandardSecondary Standard
DefinitionKnown concentration prepared directly by accurate weighingConcentration determined by standardisation against a primary standard
Purity required≥99.9%Lower purity acceptable
ExamplesPotassium hydrogen phthalate (KHP), Na₂CO₃, K₂Cr₂O₇NaOH, HCl, KMnO₄, EDTA
StabilityMust be stable indefinitely when stored properlyMay degrade; re-standardise periodically

Criteria for a primary standard

A chemical can only be used as a primary standard if it meets all of the following:

  • High purity: ≥99.9% pure, certifiable by the supplier (NIST-traceable preferred)
  • High molar mass: A large molar mass reduces the relative weighing error. Compounds with low MW require weighing such tiny masses that balance error becomes significant.
  • Stability: Must not absorb CO₂ or water from the atmosphere, and must not decompose or oxidise on storage.
  • Solubility: Must dissolve completely and rapidly in the chosen solvent, typically water.
  • Non-hygroscopic: Compounds that absorb atmospheric moisture give an incorrect mass and produce a standard with an unknown concentration.
  • Reacts stoichiometrically: For titration applications, the reaction with the titrant must be complete, fast, and well-defined.

Step-by-step preparation procedure

The following procedure applies to preparing any primary standard solution — for example, a 0.1000 M KHP solution used to standardise sodium hydroxide.

Equipment required

  • Analytical balance (4 decimal places, ± 0.1 mg)
  • Weighing boat or glass weighing dish
  • Calibrated volumetric flask (Class A — the most accurate class)
  • Wash bottle with distilled or deionised water
  • Glass funnel and stirring rod
  • Desiccator loaded with fresh silica gel desiccant
  • Oven for drying

Accurate weighing

Example — calculate mass for 250 mL of 0.1000 M KHP
1
Target: 0.1000 mol/L × 0.250 L = 0.02500 mol of KHP
2
Mass = 0.02500 mol × 204.22 g/mol = 5.1055 g
3
Dry KHP at 110°C for at least 2 hours. Transfer to desiccator, cool for 30 minutes.
4
Tare the weighing boat. Carefully weigh out approximately 5.10 g. Record the exact mass to 4 decimal places.
Record the exact mass — e.g. 5.1063 g. Use this to calculate the exact molarity of your solution: c = 5.1063 / (204.22 × 0.250) = 0.1001 M
Record the exact mass, not the target mass

Never try to weigh out exactly 5.1055 g and then report 0.1000 M as if that's exactly what you achieved. Instead, weigh as close as practical, record the actual mass, and calculate the actual molarity. This is the correct approach in quantitative analysis.

Dissolution and quantitative transfer

Quantitative transfer means moving every single molecule of the weighed compound into the volumetric flask — no losses. This is achieved by:

  • Dissolving the solid in a small volume of solvent in a beaker first, with a stirring rod
  • Pouring the solution through a glass funnel into the volumetric flask
  • Rinsing the beaker, stirring rod, and funnel at least 3 times with small portions of solvent, adding all rinses to the flask
⛔ Do not dissolve directly in the volumetric flask

Dissolving solids directly in a volumetric flask can generate heat, causing the solution to temporarily expand — meaning the flask is full before all the solvent is added. Always dissolve in a beaker first, cool to room temperature, then transfer quantitatively to the flask.

Making up to volume

With the transferred solution in the volumetric flask, add solvent to approximately 1 cm below the graduation mark. Mix by inverting and swirling several times. Then use a dropper or wash bottle to add solvent drop by drop until the bottom of the meniscus sits exactly on the graduation mark — viewed at eye level. Stopper the flask and invert 20 times to mix thoroughly.

Standardising secondary standard solutions

Secondary standards (NaOH, HCl, KMnO₄, etc.) cannot be prepared directly because they are either not pure enough, absorb atmospheric gases, or are volatile. Instead, they are prepared at an approximate concentration, then standardised by titrating them against a primary standard.

Standardising NaOH against KHP
1
Pipette exactly 25.00 mL of your KHP primary standard (c = 0.1001 M) into a conical flask. Add 2–3 drops of phenolphthalein indicator.
2
Titrate slowly with the NaOH solution from a burette. Stop at the first persistent pale pink endpoint (lasting ≥30 s).
3
Record the volume of NaOH used: e.g. 24.85 mL
4
Moles of KHP = 0.1001 × 0.02500 = 0.0025025 mol. At equivalence: moles NaOH = moles KHP (1:1 reaction).
5
c(NaOH) = 0.0025025 / 0.02485 = 0.1007 mol/L
Repeat 3 times and use the mean. Discard results where titre values differ by more than 0.10 mL.

Common mistakes and how to avoid them

MistakeConsequencePrevention
Not drying the primary standardAdsorbed water increases mass → concentration is lower than calculatedAlways dry per specified conditions; cool in desiccator
Dissolving directly in the volumetric flaskHeat expansion → incorrect final volume → wrong concentrationDissolve in a beaker, cool, then transfer
Not rinsing beaker/funnelLoss of solute → concentration is lower than calculatedRinse at least 3× with small portions of solvent
Filling above the graduation markConcentration is too low; the solution must be discardedAdd final drops carefully with a dropper; view meniscus at eye level
Using a dirty volumetric flaskContamination; film causes meniscus to stick and read incorrectlyClean with detergent, rinse 3× with distilled water; check for water beading
Using tap water as solventDissolved ions (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Cl⁻) can interfere or react with the analyteAlways use freshly distilled or deionised water for standards

Stability and storage of standard solutions

Even a perfectly prepared standard will degrade over time. Follow these guidelines:

  • NaOH: Absorbs CO₂ from air, forming Na₂CO₃. Store in a sealed, CO₂-absorber-protected bottle. Re-standardise weekly or monthly depending on required precision.
  • KMnO₄: Slowly oxidises organic matter and decomposes in light. Store in a dark amber bottle. Filter through sintered glass (not paper) before use.
  • EDTA: Relatively stable in aqueous solution. Can be stored for several months if sealed.
  • Silver nitrate (AgNO₃): Light-sensitive — store in a dark amber bottle in a dark cupboard.
  • All standards: Label with compound name, concentration (with uncertainty), preparation date, analyst initials, and expiry date. Store in a cool, dark location away from direct sunlight.

Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Primary standards must be pure (≥99.9%), stable, have high MW, and be non-hygroscopic.
  • Always dry primary standards before weighing, then cool in a desiccator.
  • Record the exact mass weighed — calculate actual molarity from what you weighed, not what you targeted.
  • Never dissolve a solid directly in the volumetric flask — heat expansion will give the wrong concentration.
  • Rinse the beaker and funnel at least 3 times; all rinses go into the flask. This is quantitative transfer.
  • Make up to volume drop-by-drop, with the meniscus at eye level, sitting exactly on the graduation mark.
  • Secondary standards (NaOH, HCl, KMnO₄) must be standardised against a primary standard — never rely on nominal concentration.
